    SAP: Privacy-Preserving Fine-Tuning on Language Models with Split-and-Privatize Framework

    International Joint Conference on Artificial Intelligence (IJCAI), 2025

    The paper proposes the Split-and-Privatize (SAP) framework, which integrates split learning and differential privacy to address privacy leakage risks in Model-as-a-Service (MaaS) based Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT). It also introduces the Contributing-Token-Identification (CTI) method to balance model utility and privacy. Experiments show that SAP achieves a 65% improvement in empirical privacy with only a 1% degradation in model performance on the Stanford Sentiment Treebank dataset, outperforming state-of-the-art baselines.

    HALUPROBE: Understanding and Detecting Hallucinations in Large Language Models Through Internal State Analysis

    arXiv preprint, 2025

    The paper introduces HALUPROBE, a framework that systematically extracts internal states from large language model inference to understand and detect hallucinations. It divides the LLM inference process into understanding, query, and generation stages, extracting 8 features to analyze hallucination sources. Experiments show that attention-based features like Lookback Ratio and Attention Entropy effectively distinguish hallucinated outputs, with the Sliced Window token selection strategy achieving up to 87% accuracy on the HaluEval dataset. The framework also explores the impact of RAG on inference and reveals limitations in feature transferability across datasets.

    Clique Gossiping

    IEEE/ACM Transactions on Networking, 2019

    The paper proposes and investigates a framework for clique gossip protocols. As complete subnetworks, the existence of cliques is ubiquitous in various social, computer, and engineering networks. By clique gossiping, nodes interact with each other along a sequence of cliques. Clique-gossip protocols are defined as arbitrary linear node interactions where node states are vectors evolving as linear dynamical systems. Such protocols become clique-gossip averaging algorithms when node states are scalars under averaging rules. The paper generalizes the classical notion of line graph to capture the essential node interaction structure induced by both the underlying network and the specific clique sequence. It proves a fundamental eigenvalue invariance principle for periodic clique-gossip protocols, which implies that any permutation of the clique sequence leads to the same spectrum for the overall state transition when the generalized line graph contains no cycle. The paper also proves that for a network with n nodes, cliques with smaller sizes determined by factors of n can always be constructed leading to finite-time convergent clique-gossip averaging algorithms, provided n is not a prime number. Additionally, the acceleration effects of clique-gossiping are illustrated via numerical examples.

    Network Flows that Solve Least Squares for Linear Equations

    Automatica, 2020

    The paper presents a first-order distributed continuous-time algorithm for computing the least-squares solution to a linear equation over networks. It provides convergence results for fixed graphs when the solution is unique, using a nonintegrable and diminishing step size, and establishes the exact rate of convergence for various step size choices. For non-unique solutions, convergence to one such solution is proved for constantly connected switching graphs with square integrable step size, and for uniformly jointly connected switching graphs under boundedness of system states. Numerical examples validate the results and illustrate the impact of step size on convergence speed.

    An Arrow-Hurwicz-Uzawa Type Flow as Least Squares Solver for Network Linear Equations

    Automatica, 2019

    The paper presents distributed continuous and discrete-time algorithms for solving least squares problems in networked linear equations. It introduces an Arrow-Hurwicz-Uzawa type flow, establishing necessary and sufficient conditions for exponential convergence to the least squares solution. The framework is validated across various graph structures (path, star, ring graphs) and demonstrates surprising performance in switching networks, where high-frequency switching enables approximate solutions even when individual graphs fail. Numerical examples confirm exponential convergence rates under the derived conditions.
